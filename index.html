Chasing Zero

A hands-on introduction to digital PID control loops

Every time your 3D printer heats its nozzle, a small program is running in a tight loop. It reads the current temperature, compares it to the target, and decides how much power to send to the heater — then does it again, dozens of times per second.

This is a control loop. The goal is always the same: close the gap between where you are and where you want to be. To chase the error down to zero.

Getting this right is harder than it sounds. Apply too little power and you never quite reach temperature. Apply too much and you overshoot, then oscillate. The algorithm that solves this cleanly is called a PID controller — short for Proportional, Integral, Derivative. We will build one up, one term at a time.

Key concepts

Setpoint — the target value you want to reach (e.g. 200 °C).
Process variable — the value being measured (current temperature).
Error — the gap between them: error = setpoint − temperature.
Output — the control signal sent to the actuator (heater power, 0–100 %).
Gain — a tuning coefficient that scales each term's influence.

Each simulation on this page models a typical FDM printer hotend. You set the target temperature and the starting temperature; everything else — how fast the heater raises temperature, how fast the hotend loses heat to the air — is fixed to realistic values and hidden away.

1 — Proportional Control

The most natural idea: if you're far from the target, apply a lot of power. If you're close, apply less. Make the output proportional to the error. 

error  = targetTemp - currentTemp;
output = Kp * error;             // proportional term only
output = clamp(output, 0, 100); // heater can't go below 0% or above 100%

Kp is the proportional gain. Raise it and the controller responds more aggressively to error. Lower it and it responds more gently.

This works well at first — the heater blasts at full power while the temperature is far off. But there's a catch. As the temperature approaches the setpoint, the error shrinks, and so does the output. Eventually the controller reaches a balance point where the output is just enough to overcome heat loss — but that balance is always below the setpoint. The controller has no reason to push further. This leftover gap is called steady-state error.

A high Kp can also cause the temperature to overshoot — it drives hard past the target and then has to correct back, leading to oscillation.

Need a hint?

Start with Kp = 2. Notice how the temperature levels off just below the setpoint — that is steady-state error. Then push Kp to 5 or higher and watch the temperature swing past the target.

2 — Integral Control

The steady-state error from Section 1 happens because proportional control only reacts to the current error. Once the error is small, the output is small — even if the temperature has been sitting below target for a long time.

The fix is to accumulate error over time. This is the integral. Every cycle the temperature is below target, the integral grows a little. That growth adds extra output, providing the push needed to close the gap all the way to zero. 

error     = targetTemp - currentTemp;
integral += error;                        // sum up error over time
output    = Kp * error + Ki * integral;  // P + I terms
output    = clamp(output, 0, 100);

Ki is the integral gain. A well-tuned Ki eliminates steady-state error entirely. But if Ki is too large, the accumulated integral can grow enormous before the temperature catches up — then it dumps all of that stored energy at once, rocketing the temperature well past the setpoint. This is called integral windup.

What does the I term display mean?

The box to the right of the graph shows how much heater output the integral term is commanding on its own, as a percentage. The heater is capped at 100% — but the integral doesn't know that. It just keeps accumulating.

When the display turns red, the integral is demanding more than the heater can physically deliver. All that extra demand is stored up and keeps the heater pinned at full power long after the temperature has blown past the setpoint. That is integral windup.

Need a hint?

Start with Kp = 2, Ki = 0.05. The temperature now reaches the setpoint. Then push Ki to 0.3 or higher to see integral windup — watch the I term display turn red and the temperature rocket past the target.

3 — Derivative Control

Even with a PI controller, the temperature can overshoot if the integral winds up or the gains are aggressive. What we need is a way to anticipate where the system is heading and start braking before it overshoots.

The derivative term does exactly this. It measures the rate of change of the error — how fast the error is shrinking. When the temperature is racing toward the setpoint, the derivative applies a countering force to slow the approach. Think of it as the controller asking: "even if I'm not at the setpoint yet, am I approaching it fast enough that I should ease off?" 

error      = targetTemp - currentTemp;
integral  += error;
derivative = error - prevError;          // rate of change of error
output     = Kp * error
           + Ki * integral
           + Kd * derivative;           // full P + I + D
output     = clamp(output, 0, 100);
prevError  = error;                     // remember for next cycle

Kd is the derivative gain. It is the most sensitive of the three terms. Too much Kd slows the response unnecessarily; in a real system it can also amplify measurement noise and cause erratic heater behaviour.

With all three terms tuned together you can achieve a response that reaches the setpoint quickly, settles cleanly without oscillation, and holds it there with no steady-state error. This is the complete PID controller.

Tuning guide

Not sure where to start, or why your graph looks wrong? Match what you're seeing to the symptoms below.

The temperature barely moves

Kp is too low. Proportional gain is what gets the heater moving in the first place — without it, the other two terms can't do much either. Raise Kp until you see a meaningful response.

The temperature climbs but stops short of the target and stays there

This is steady-state error — proportional control doing exactly what it's designed to do, and hitting its limit. Add a small Ki to close the remaining gap.

The temperature shoots past the target and oscillates

Either Kp is too high, or Ki has caused windup. Lower Kp first. If the I term display was red during the run, lower Ki too. Once the oscillation is under control, add a small Kd to dampen the swings.

The temperature overshoots once, then settles slowly

This is actually fine behaviour. If you want it to settle faster without more overshoot, raise Kd slightly. If it starts oscillating again, you've gone too far.

The I term display turns red immediately

Ki is too large. The integral is accumulating faster than the heater can act on it. Bring Ki down until the display stays below 100% during a normal run.

The temperature oscillates rapidly with very short peaks and valleys

Kd is too high. The derivative term is overreacting to small changes in error and fighting itself. Reduce Kd, or set it to zero and re-tune Kp and Ki before adding it back.

General approach when starting from scratch

  1. Set Ki and Kd to zero.
  2. Raise Kp until the temperature reaches the setpoint — accept some overshoot for now.
  3. Add a small Ki to eliminate any remaining gap below the target.
  4. Add a small Kd to smooth out overshoot. Increase gradually.
  5. Fine-tune all three until the response looks clean.
